# Civic AI Tools & Typed Standards
*Open infrastructure for civic analysis that can be independently verified.*

**The problem.** Public information is getting harder to trust and harder to act on. As AI-generated content spreads, the question about any claim is shifting from whether it is well-argued to whether it can be confirmed as real at all: that it came from the data it cites, unaltered. Most AI tools deepen this by hiding what they actually did to produce an answer. And for the people who bear real consequences when a claim proves false — auditors, regulators, large-organization decision-makers, courts — the ability to check it is a requirement, not a convenience. The same gap appears as a reproducibility problem across science, journalism, and policy: AI-assisted results that cannot be retraced to their inputs, their method, or even the model that produced them.

**The approach.** Two pieces of open infrastructure.

- **Civic AI Tools** gives non-technical users a natural-language interface to real civic datasets that reports only what live queries return, with every figure traceable to the query behind it. A reporter, public servant, or analyst gets a grounded, re-runnable answer instead of a confident fabrication.
- **Typed Standards** lets any analysis be published as a cryptographically signed, timestamped, provenance-graphed package that anyone can verify independently, without trusting the author. A second party can confirm it came from the stated data, unaltered, using different software. This already works today.

Together they give institutions that still hold public trust a way to make identifiable, independently checkable claims as synthetic content multiplies.

**What is proven, and what is being tested.** What can be demonstrated now: grounded access, integrity a third party can verify, and interoperability across independent implementations. What remains a hypothesis, and is stated as one: that verifiable analyses actually get verified, that verification shifts trust, and that this measurably improves how institutions act on information and reproduce each other's work. These questions cannot be studied until the infrastructure exists, is inexpensive, and is in use. Building it is what turns them from debate into experiment.

**The bet.** Trustworthy, low-cost, independently checkable civic analysis is likely a prerequisite for real progress on trust, accountability, and reproducibility. For that to be tested at any meaningful scale, it has to be neutral, open infrastructure rather than a platform people are simply asked to trust. If the value holds, the natural next step is that some uses begin to require verification of this kind: an organization making it a condition for its own data-driven decisions, or an auditor, newsroom, or court treating it as a baseline for what counts as evidence. That is the test worth running, and the reason to build the infrastructure now.
